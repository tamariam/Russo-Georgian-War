August War

The 2008 Russo-Georgian War also named as August war was a war between Georgia, on one side, and Russia and the Russian-backed self-proclaimed republics of South Ossetia and Abkhazia, on the other.

Pre War

What caused the conflict?

Background Tensions:

The roots of the conflict can be traced back to the collapse of the Soviet Union in 1991 when Georgia declared independence. The breakaway regions of Abkhazia and South Ossetia sought independence from Georgia, resulting in ongoing tensions and sporadic outbreaks of violence.

Separatist Conflicts:

Abkhazia and South Ossetia, both located within Georgia's internationally recognized borders, declared independence in the early 1990s. Russia supported the separatist movements in these regions, leading to a de facto control of Abkhazia and South Ossetia by Russian-backed separatist governments.

NATO Aspirations:

Georgia, under the leadership of President Mikheil Saakashvili, pursued closer ties with the West, including aspirations to join NATO. This move was met with resistance from Russia, which saw it as a threat to its influence in the region.

Increasing Tensions:

In the years leading up to the war, there were numerous incidents along the borders of Abkhazia and South Ossetia, including sporadic clashes between Georgian forces and separatist militias. Russia gradually increased its military presence in the breakaway regions, reinforcing its support for the separatists.

Early August

In August 2008, a series of incidents took place that escalated into a full-scale war. Georgian forces launched an offensive to regain control over South Ossetia, leading to a Russian military intervention. Russian forces quickly overwhelmed the Georgian military, and fighting spread to other parts of Georgia, including Abkhazia.

pre war

Day 1: August 7, 2008

Georgian forces launched an offensive to regain control over the breakaway region of South Ossetia. Heavy fighting broke out in the South Ossetian capital, Tskhinvali, between Georgian forces and separatist militias. Russia began deploying military forces, including tanks and artillery, to support the separatist forces in South Ossetia. Day 2: August 8, 2008

Day 2: August 8, 2008

Russian airstrikes targeted Georgian military installations and infrastructure in various parts of Georgia, including the capital city, Tbilisi. Russian forces continued to advance into South Ossetia, pushing back Georgian troops.

Day 3: August 9, 2008

Russian forces expanded the conflict beyond South Ossetia, launching attacks in other parts of Georgia, including the strategically important city of Gori. The Georgian government declared a state of war and called for international support.

Day 4: August 10, 2008

The conflict intensified as both sides engaged in heavy fighting across various fronts. Russian forces entered the breakaway region of Abkhazia, further expanding their military operations.

Day 5: August 11-12, 2008

The international community intensified its efforts to broker a ceasefire and resolve the conflict diplomatically. On August 12, a ceasefire agreement was reached, putting an end to the active phase of the war. Russian and Georgian military forces halted their operations.It's important to note that the conflict did not end with the ceasefire agreement, and tensions remained high. Following the war, Russia recognized the independence of both South Ossetia and Abkhazia, and Russian forces maintained a military presence in the regions. The war had a significant impact on the geopolitical dynamics in the region and strained relations between Russia, Georgia, and the West.
